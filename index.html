Marathon distance

The official marathon distance is 42.195 km (26.2 miles), standardized in 1921. Pace calculators divide total time by this distance to get average speed per km or mile.

Pace = Finish Time ÷ Distance
4:00:00 ÷ 42.195 km = 5:41/km

Even vs negative splits

  • Even pace — same split every mile/km; simplest for first marathons
  • Negative split — run second half faster; saves glycogen early
  • Most elites negative split; beginners often positive split from starting too fast

Using split tables

  • Check wrist at each 5K or mile marker
  • Adjust ±5 sec/km for hills or wind
  • Half-marathon split should be ~50% of goal time
  • Bank 15–30 sec buffer for miles 20–26

FAQ

What pace do I need for a 4-hour marathon?

A 4:00:00 marathon requires an average pace of 5:41 per km (9:09 per mile). Your half-marathon checkpoint should be around 1:59:30. Add 10–15 seconds per km in the first 5K to avoid going out too fast.

How do I convert marathon pace between min/km and min/mile?

Multiply min/km by 1.60934 to get min/mile, or divide min/mile by 1.60934 for min/km. Example: 5:00/km ≈ 8:03/mi. This calculator shows both automatically.

What is a negative split marathon?

A negative split means running the second half faster than the first. Many coaches recommend starting 1–3% slower than goal pace, then accelerating after halfway. This calculator's negative split mode adds ~2% to first-half splits and subtracts ~2% from second-half splits.

How accurate are marathon pace calculators?

Pace math is exact — splits are pure time ÷ distance. Real race day varies with hills, weather, crowding, and fitness. Use splits as a guide; adjust on feel, especially after mile 20 (km 32).

What half-marathon time predicts marathon fitness?

Rule of thumb: double your half-marathon time and add 10–20 minutes for a marathon estimate (e.g. 1:45 half → ~3:40–3:50 marathon). This assumes proper marathon training — not just half-marathon fitness.

When should I start my race-day pace?

Hold back the first 1–2 km (or mile). Most runners who hit the wall started 15–30 sec/km too fast. Aim to reach halfway within 15–30 seconds of even-split projection, then hold or push if you feel strong.
